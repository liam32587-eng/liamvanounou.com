The 8-Week Structure

Competition prep is about managing what you already have — not trying to add new skills. This is about sharpening, stabilizing, and peaking at the right time.

Week 1–2

Baseline & Stabilization

Assess current skill consistency. Identify weaknesses in routines. Lock in what's already clean before adding anything. High volume, moderate intensity.

Week 3–4

Intensity Block

Highest intensity of the 8 weeks. Full routines with connection, difficulty, and artistry. This is where you push hardest — when there's still time to recover.

Week 5–6

Sharpening

Begin reducing volume. Focus on execution quality — every detail, every form element. Start competition simulation: full sets with judging eye.

Week 7

Taper

Volume drops significantly. Intensity stays high in short bursts. No new skills. The goal is to feel fresh, confident, and sharp — not tired and worked.

Week 8

Competition Week

Light training only. Full rest days built in. Mental preparation, visualization, and competition day protocol. Arrive ready — not worn down.

Competition Mindset

Mental Performance

Visualization

Every elite gymnast visualizes. Not as a vague hope — as a specific, detailed, sensory rehearsal of every element in every routine.

How to Do It

  • 10 min per day in the final 3 weeks before competition
  • Close eyes, visualize every routine start to finish
  • Include the feeling of each skill — not just the image
  • Visualize the crowd, the judges, the chalk, the sounds
  • Always end with a perfect landing — program success
Mental Performance

Arousal Control

Competition nerves are energy. The goal is not to eliminate them — it's to channel them. The right arousal level makes you sharper, not worse.

How to Do It

  • If too anxious: box breathing (4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold)
  • If too flat: power poses, music, aggressive self-talk
  • Find your optimal zone in training simulations
  • Pre-routine routine: same every time, builds confidence
  • Nervousness = readiness. Reframe it before you walk on
Focus

Process Over Outcome

Thinking about your score mid-routine destroys performance. Your only focus during competition is the next skill — nothing else exists.

How to Do It

  • Define 2–3 key cues per routine — not 20
  • Practice cue-based focus in every training run
  • After a mistake: reset, do not ruminate
  • Score doesn't matter until you've finished all 6
  • The routine in front of you is the only one that exists
Consistency

Routine Consistency

A D-score 0.2 higher means nothing if you fall twice. Consistent, clean execution beats ambitious difficulty every time at most levels.

How to Do It

  • Know your hit rate on every element over 10 attempts
  • If below 80% hit rate — simplify for competition
  • Count your successful runs in training, not just attempts
  • Target: 8/10 full routines clean in the final 2 weeks
  • If you're not hitting it in training, you won't hit it on the floor

Competition Day Protocol

Morning

Wake up at your normal time. Light breakfast — nothing heavy. 20 min of visualization. Review your 2–3 cues per routine. No new information today.

Warm-Up

Same warm-up as always — no surprises. Consistent heart rate elevation, then apparatus-specific prep. Light skills only — save energy for the competition floor.

Between Events

Breathe. Eat light carbs. Stay warm. Don't analyze the last routine — it's done. Reset to the next event. Talk to your coach, not your competitors.

After Competition

Regardless of result: protein and carbs within 30 minutes. Light mobility work. Write down what you'll work on — then let the result go. One competition is one data point.
